The Brief

Real differentiators.
No website to show them.

Sterling had what most rental companies don't — SIOTO Safety Seal certification, upfront transparent pricing, and a crew that actually shows up. What they didn't have was a website that communicated any of that. The goal was to build a site that earns trust before the phone rings, shows up where buyers search (including AI tools like ChatGPT and Google AI Overviews), and converts browsers into booked events.

Customer Research

Built on what buyers actually think.

Before a single page was written, research mapped the pain points, decision triggers, and objections that determine whether a buyer books or keeps looking. Every copy and architecture decision traces back to something specific in that research.

Top Pain Points

  • Vendor doesn't show up on time
  • Hidden fees, pricing not clear upfront
  • Equipment arrives dirty or damaged
  • Hard to get a quote or a response
  • Unclear cancellation terms
  • Weather anxiety for outdoor events
  • Permit confusion
  • Date availability pressure in peak season

Decision Triggers

  • Date urgency — peak weekends book out
  • Transparent pricing that matches the invoice
  • Licensed, insured, and independently certified
  • Delivery and setup handled — no self-assembly
  • Fast response time to a quote request
  • Reviews mentioning punctuality
  • Clear $500 minimum — removes hesitation
  • Simple booking process

Objections Addressed

  • "It's too expensive" → anchored vs. buying once for one event
  • "Do you serve my area?" → suburb names throughout, not just one page
  • "Bad experience before" → verifiable credentials, not just claims
  • "What if it rains?" → weather policy on every outdoor page
  • "I don't know what I need" → guide-first content reduces friction

Copy Approach

Every word tied to research.

The copy brief started with one question: what is the person reading this page actually worried about right now? These four principles governed every page.

01

Specific Numbers Over Vague Claims

Buyers don't trust adjectives. They trust data. Specific figures appear everywhere a decision is being made.

"$500 minimum · 50% deposit holds your date · 21-day cancellation window · 21–30 days for corporate · 12–18 months for weddings"
02

Address the Objection at the Moment It Arises

The weather policy isn't on a FAQ page you have to find. It's in the tent page, the outdoor planning guide, the packages page, and the contact page — because that's when the buyer is thinking about it.

03

No Filler Language

A specific list of phrases was excluded from the entire site: "hassle-free," "premier," "best in Chicago," "above and beyond," "seamless experience." Every sentence that wanted to say we're the best was rewritten to prove it instead.

04

Three Audiences, One Page

Each service page has a "Who this is for" section that speaks to corporate planners, wedding couples, and private hosts — in priority order — without making any of them feel like an afterthought.

Corporate → credibility and invoicing. Weddings → weather policy and setup. Private hosts → transparent pricing and low pressure.
